4  Bilinear Form

Definition. An estimator \(\tilde{s}(X, Y) = X^T A Y\) for \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}\) is called bilinear.

Expanding using \(X = \theta_x + \varepsilon_x\) and \(Y = \theta_y + \varepsilon_y\) and taking expectations gives

\[ \mathbb{E}[\tilde{s}(X, Y)] = \theta_x^T A \theta_y + \operatorname{tr}(A)\, \sigma_{xy}. \]

For unbiasedness, \(A\) must satisfy

  1. \(\theta_x^T A \theta_y = 0\) for all \(\theta_x, \theta_y \in \Theta_L\), and
  2. \(\operatorname{tr}(A) = 1\).

To reduce the search space, we impose a symmetry condition. Let \(C_k\) be the circulant shift matrix, which shifts a vector \(k\) positions to the right circularly.

Definition. A bilinear estimator is equivariant if \(\tilde{s}(C_k X, C_k Y) = \tilde{s}(X, Y)\) for all \(k\).

Lemma (Equivariant \(\Leftrightarrow\) Symmetric Circulant). Since \(\tilde{s}\) is a covariance estimator, \(A\) must be symmetric. The bilinear estimator \(X^T A Y\) is equivariant if and only if \(A\) is a symmetric circulant: \[ A = \operatorname{circ}(a_0, a_1, \ldots, a_{\lfloor n/2 \rfloor}, \ldots, a_1) = \begin{bmatrix} a_0 & a_1 & \cdots & a_1 \\ a_1 & a_0 & \cdots & \vdots \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ a_1 & \cdots & a_1 & a_0 \end{bmatrix}, \] where \(a_k = a_{n-k}\) for all \(k\).

Proof. Since \(\tilde{s}(C_k X, C_k Y) = X^T(C_k^T A C_k)Y\), equivariance holds iff \(A = C_k^T A C_k\) for all \(k\), which is equivalent to \(A\) being circulant. Symmetry of \(A\) further requires \(a_k = a_{n-k}\), so \(A\) is a symmetric circulant. \(\square\)

This reduces the \(n^2\) free parameters of \(A\) to the \(n\) entries of its first row. Applying the unbiasedness constraints then uniquely determines \(A\), and the resulting estimator is exactly \(\hat{\sigma}_{xy}\).

4.1 Properties of Circulant Matrices

Let \(C_1 = \operatorname{circ}(0, 1, 0, \ldots, 0)\) be the elementary shift-by-one matrix, so \(C_1^k = C_k\). The following facts hold:

  1. Power. \(C_k = C_1^k\), so applying a shift of \(k\) is the same as applying a shift of 1 exactly \(k\) times. As a consequence, every circulant matrix is a polynomial in \(C_1\): \(\operatorname{circ}(a_0, \ldots, a_{n-1}) = \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} a_k C_1^k\).

  2. Transpose. \(C_k^T = C_{n-k} = C_k^{-1}\), since \(C_k\) is a permutation matrix.

  3. Circulant form. \(C_k = \operatorname{circ}(e_k)\), where \(e_k\) is the standard basis vector with a 1 in position \(k\) and 0s elsewhere. That is, \(C_k\) is the circulant matrix whose first row has a single 1 on the \(k\)-th band.

4.2 Solving for \(A\)

Since \(A\) is a symmetric circulant, it is determined by \((a_0, \ldots, a_{\lfloor n/2 \rfloor})\). The unbiasedness conditions reduce to a linear system in these entries.

For a circulant \(A\), the condition \(\theta_x^T A \theta_y = 0\) for all piecewise-constant \(\theta_x, \theta_y \in \Theta_L\) is equivalent to requiring that the sum of all entries of every \(\ell \times \ell\) contiguous submatrix of \(A\) is zero, for \(\ell = L, \ldots, \lfloor n/2 \rfloor\). Denoting by \(A_\ell\) such a submatrix, this reads

\[ \|A_\ell\|_1 = 0, \qquad \ell = L, \ldots, \lfloor n/2 \rfloor. \]

For a symmetric circulant, this sum depends only on \((a_0, \ldots, a_{\lfloor n/2 \rfloor})\) and takes the explicit form

\[ g(\ell) = \ell\, a_0 + \sum_{i=1}^{\min\{\lfloor n/2 \rfloor,\, \ell\}} 2(\ell - i)\, a_i + \sum_{i=\lfloor n/2 \rfloor + 1}^{\ell} 2(\ell - i)\, a_{n-i}. \]

For \(L = 2\), setting \(g(\ell) = 0\) for \(\ell = 2, 3\) and using \(a_0 = 1/n\) gives

\[ g(2) = 2a_0 + 2a_1 = 0 \implies a_1 = -a_0, \] \[ g(3) = 3a_0 + 4a_1 + 2a_2 = 0 \implies a_2 = \tfrac{a_0}{2}, \]

and for \(\ell \geq 4\) one shows by induction that \(a_{\ell - 1} = 0\). Together with \(a_0 = 1/n\), this uniquely determines \(A\).

4.3 Matrix Form of ECE

Proposition. For \(L = 2\), the ECE \(\hat{\sigma}_{xy} = X^T A Y\) where \(A = \frac{1}{n}\operatorname{circ}(a)\) and

\[ a = \left(1,\; -1,\; \tfrac{1}{2},\; 0,\; \ldots,\; 0,\; \tfrac{1}{2},\; -1\right). \]

Proof. Since \(T_k(X,Y) = ((I-C_k)X)^T((I-C_k)Y)\), we have \(T_k(X,Y) = X^T A_k Y\) where

\[ A_k = (I - C_k)^T(I - C_k) = 2I - C_k - C_k^T. \]

Since \(C_k = \operatorname{circ}(e_k)\), we have \(A_k = \operatorname{circ}(\tilde{a}_k)\) where the first row \(\tilde{a}_k\) has \(+2\) at position \(0\), \(-1\) at position \(k\), \(-1\) at position \(n-k\), and \(0\) elsewhere.

Substituting into \(\hat{\sigma}_{xy} = \frac{1}{2n}(2T_1 - T_2)\),

\[ A = \frac{1}{2n}(2A_1 - A_2) = \frac{1}{2n}\bigl(2(2I - C_1 - C_1^T) - (2I - C_2 - C_2^T)\bigr) = \frac{1}{n}\operatorname{circ}(a). \quad\square \]

4.4 Derivation of \(\mathbb{E}[X^T A Y]\)

Substituting \(X = \theta_x + \varepsilon_x\) and \(Y = \theta_y + \varepsilon_y\),

\[ X^T A Y = (\theta_x + \varepsilon_x)^T A (\theta_y + \varepsilon_y) = \theta_x^T A \theta_y + \theta_x^T A \varepsilon_y + \varepsilon_x^T A \theta_y + \varepsilon_x^T A \varepsilon_y. \]

Taking expectations and using \(\mathbb{E}[\varepsilon_x] = \mathbb{E}[\varepsilon_y] = 0\),

\[ \mathbb{E}[X^T A Y] = \theta_x^T A \theta_y + \theta_x^T A \mathbb{E}[\varepsilon_y] + \mathbb{E}[\varepsilon_x]^T A \theta_y + \mathbb{E}[\varepsilon_x^T A \varepsilon_y] = \theta_x^T A \theta_y + \mathbb{E}[\varepsilon_x^T A \varepsilon_y]. \]

For the remaining term, since \(\varepsilon_x^T A \varepsilon_y\) is a scalar,

\[ \varepsilon_x^T A \varepsilon_y = \operatorname{tr}(\varepsilon_x^T A \varepsilon_y). \]

By the cyclic property of trace, \(\operatorname{tr}(\varepsilon_x^T A \varepsilon_y) = \operatorname{tr}(A \varepsilon_y \varepsilon_x^T)\). Taking the expectation and applying linearity,

\[ \mathbb{E}[\varepsilon_x^T A \varepsilon_y] = \operatorname{tr}(A\, \mathbb{E}[\varepsilon_y \varepsilon_x^T]) = \operatorname{tr}(A \cdot \sigma_{xy} I) = \sigma_{xy}\operatorname{tr}(A), \]

where we used \(\mathbb{E}[\varepsilon_y \varepsilon_x^T] = \sigma_{xy} I\).

Therefore,

\[ \mathbb{E}[X^T A Y] = \theta_x^T A \theta_y + \sigma_{xy}\operatorname{tr}(A). \qquad \square \]

4.5 Derivation of \(\operatorname{Var}(X^T A Y)\)

Using the same decomposition, write \(X^T A Y = \theta_x^T A \theta_y + \theta_x^T A \varepsilon_y + \varepsilon_x^T A \theta_y + \varepsilon_x^T A \varepsilon_y\). Since the first term is a constant, it does not contribute to the variance, so

\[ \operatorname{Var}(X^T A Y) = \operatorname{Var}(\theta_x^T A \varepsilon_y + \varepsilon_x^T A \theta_y + \varepsilon_x^T A \varepsilon_y). \]

Expanding,

\[\begin{align*} \operatorname{Var}(X^T A Y) &= \operatorname{Var}(\theta_x^T A \varepsilon_y) + \operatorname{Var}(\varepsilon_x^T A \theta_y) + \operatorname{Var}(\varepsilon_x^T A \varepsilon_y) \\ &\quad + 2\operatorname{Cov}(\theta_x^T A \varepsilon_y,\, \varepsilon_x^T A \theta_y) \\ &\quad + 2\operatorname{Cov}(\theta_x^T A \varepsilon_y,\, \varepsilon_x^T A \varepsilon_y) \\ &\quad + 2\operatorname{Cov}(\varepsilon_x^T A \theta_y,\, \varepsilon_x^T A \varepsilon_y). \end{align*}\]

Derivation of each term

Term 1: \(\operatorname{Var}(\theta_x^T A \varepsilon_y)\). Since \(\theta_x^T A\) is a fixed row vector, this is the variance of a linear form in \(\varepsilon_y\):

\[ \operatorname{Var}(\theta_x^T A \varepsilon_y) = \theta_x^T A \operatorname{Var}(\varepsilon_y) A^T \theta_x = \sigma_y^2\, \theta_x^T A A^T \theta_x = \sigma_y^2\, \theta_x^T A^2 \theta_x, \]

where the last equality uses symmetry of \(A\).

Term 2: \(\operatorname{Var}(\varepsilon_x^T A \theta_y)\). By the same argument, \(A \theta_y\) is a fixed vector, so

\[ \operatorname{Var}(\varepsilon_x^T A \theta_y) = \theta_y^T A^T \operatorname{Var}(\varepsilon_x) A \theta_y = \sigma_x^2\, \theta_y^T A^2 \theta_y. \]

Term 3: \(\operatorname{Var}(\varepsilon_x^T A \varepsilon_y)\). Apply the eigendecomposition \(A = Q\Lambda Q^T\) and let \(z_x = Q^T \varepsilon_x / \sigma_x\), \(z_y = Q^T \varepsilon_y / \sigma_y\), so that \(z_x, z_y \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)\) with \(\operatorname{Cov}(z_x, z_y) = \rho I\) where \(\rho = \sigma_{xy}/(\sigma_x \sigma_y)\). Applying the eigendecomposition,

\[ \varepsilon_x^T A \varepsilon_y = \sigma_x \sigma_y\, z_x^T \Lambda z_y = \sigma_x \sigma_y \sum_i \lambda_i z_{x,i} z_{y,i}. \]

Since \(\operatorname{Cov}(z_x, z_y) = \rho I\) is diagonal, the pairs \((z_{x,i}, z_{y,i})\) are independent across \(i\), so

\[ \operatorname{Var}(\varepsilon_x^T A \varepsilon_y) = \sigma_x^2 \sigma_y^2 \sum_i \lambda_i^2\, \operatorname{Var}(z_{x,i} z_{y,i}). \]

For each \(i\), \((z_{x,i}, z_{y,i})\) is a standard bivariate Gaussian with correlation \(\rho\), so \(\operatorname{Var}(z_{x,i} z_{y,i}) = 1 + \rho^2\) (see Appendix). Therefore,

\[ \operatorname{Var}(\varepsilon_x^T A \varepsilon_y) = \sigma_x^2 \sigma_y^2 (1 + \rho^2)\operatorname{tr}(A^2) = (\sigma_x^2\sigma_y^2 + \sigma_{xy}^2)\operatorname{tr}(A^2). \]

Term 4: \(\operatorname{Cov}(\theta_x^T A \varepsilon_y,\, \varepsilon_x^T A \theta_y)\). Since \(\mathbb{E}[\varepsilon_x] = \mathbb{E}[\varepsilon_y] = 0\), both terms have zero expectation, so the covariance reduces to

\[ \operatorname{Cov}(\theta_x^T A \varepsilon_y,\, \varepsilon_x^T A \theta_y) = \mathbb{E}[(\theta_x^T A \varepsilon_y)(\varepsilon_x^T A \theta_y)] = \theta_x^T A\, \mathbb{E}[\varepsilon_y \varepsilon_x^T]\, A^T \theta_y = \sigma_{xy}\, \theta_x^T A^2 \theta_y, \]

where we used \(\mathbb{E}[\varepsilon_y \varepsilon_x^T] = \sigma_{xy} I\) and symmetry of \(A\).

Terms 5 and 6: \(\operatorname{Cov}(\theta_x^T A \varepsilon_y,\, \varepsilon_x^T A \varepsilon_y)\) and \(\operatorname{Cov}(\varepsilon_x^T A \theta_y,\, \varepsilon_x^T A \varepsilon_y)\). Since all terms have zero expectation, both covariances reduce to expectations of products involving one linear and one bilinear form in \((\varepsilon_x, \varepsilon_y)\). These are third-order moments of the zero-mean jointly Gaussian vector \((\varepsilon_x, \varepsilon_y)\), and all odd-order moments of a zero-mean Gaussian vanish. Therefore,

\[ \operatorname{Cov}(\theta_x^T A \varepsilon_y,\, \varepsilon_x^T A \varepsilon_y) = 0, \qquad \operatorname{Cov}(\varepsilon_x^T A \theta_y,\, \varepsilon_x^T A \varepsilon_y) = 0. \]

Collecting all terms,

\[ \operatorname{Var}(X^T A Y) = \sigma_y^2\, \theta_x^T A^2 \theta_x + \sigma_x^2\, \theta_y^T A^2 \theta_y + 2\sigma_{xy}\, \theta_x^T A^2 \theta_y + (\sigma_x^2\sigma_y^2 + \sigma_{xy}^2)\operatorname{tr}(A^2). \]

The noise contribution \((\sigma_x^2\sigma_y^2 + \sigma_{xy}^2)\operatorname{tr}(A^2) = O(1/n)\) by the lower bound on \(\operatorname{tr}(A^2)\) (see Appendix B), which shows the estimator is consistent.

4.6 Solving for \(A\) (\(L > 2\))

For \(L > 2\), the unbiasedness constraints leave a multi-dimensional solution space for \(a\), so we have freedom to optimize. The full variance is

\[ \operatorname{Var}(X^T A Y) = \sigma_y^2\, \theta_x^T A^2 \theta_x + \sigma_x^2\, \theta_y^T A^2 \theta_y + 2\sigma_{xy}\, \theta_x^T A^2 \theta_y + (\sigma_x^2\sigma_y^2 + \sigma_{xy}^2)\operatorname{tr}(A^2). \]

Since \(\theta_x\) and \(\theta_y\) are unknown, the signal-dependent terms cannot be minimized directly. The only term we can control is the noise term \((\sigma_x^2\sigma_y^2 + \sigma_{xy}^2)\operatorname{tr}(A^2)\), which for fixed noise levels reduces to minimizing \(\operatorname{tr}(A^2)\). This gives the optimization problem

\[ \min_{a \in \mathbb{R}^n} \operatorname{tr}(A^2) \quad \text{subject to} \quad \|A_\ell\|_1 = 0,\ \ell = L, \ldots, \lfloor n/2 \rfloor, n, \quad \operatorname{tr}(A) = 1, \]

where \(A = \operatorname{circ}(a)\).

Reducing to a norm minimization. Since \(A = \operatorname{circ}(a)\) is a circulant, every row of \(A\) is a cyclic permutation of \(a\) and therefore has the same squared norm \(\|a\|^2\). The Frobenius norm of \(A\) is thus

\[ \|A\|_F^2 = n\|a\|^2. \]

Since \(A\) is symmetric, \(\operatorname{tr}(A^2) = \operatorname{tr}(A^T A) = \|A\|_F^2 = n\|a\|^2\). Minimizing \(\operatorname{tr}(A^2)\) is therefore equivalent to minimizing \(\|a\|^2\). Because the unbiasedness constraints are linear in \(a\), the problem reduces to

\[ \min_{a \in \mathbb{R}^n} \|a\|^2 \quad \text{subject to} \quad \|A_\ell\|_1 = 0,\ \ell = L, \ldots, \lfloor n/2 \rfloor, n, \quad \operatorname{tr}(A) = 1, \]

where \(A = \operatorname{circ}(a)\). Both sets of constraints are linear in \(a\), so this is a quadratic program with a unique solution — the minimum-norm point in the feasible affine subspace.

4.7 Regression Approach

A natural way to estimate \(\sigma_{xy}\) is to exploit the linear relationship \(\mathbb{E}[T_k(X,Y)] = 2n\sigma_{xy} + k\,\omega_{xy}\) (established in the Lagged Differences chapter). Stacking \(k = 1, \ldots, L\) gives a linear model

\[ \mathbb{E}[R] = K\beta, \qquad K = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 \\ \vdots & \vdots \\ 1 & L \end{bmatrix} \in \mathbb{R}^{L \times 2}, \quad \beta = \begin{pmatrix} 2n\sigma_{xy} \\ \omega_{xy} \end{pmatrix}, \]

where \(R_k = T_k(X,Y)\). The OLS estimate is \(\hat\beta = (K^TK)^{-1}K^T R\), and the regression estimator of \(\sigma_{xy}\) is its first component rescaled:

\[ \hat\sigma_{\mathrm{reg}} = \frac{1}{2n}\, e_1^T(K^TK)^{-1}K^T R = H_{1\cdot} R, \quad H = \frac{1}{2n}(K^TK)^{-1}K^T \in \mathbb{R}^{2 \times L}, \]

where \(H_{1\cdot}\) denotes the first row of \(H\).

Relationship \(T_k(X,Y) = X^T A_k Y\). Since

\[ T_k(X,Y) = \sum_{i=1}^n (X_i - X_{i+k})(Y_i - Y_{i+k}) = \bigl((I - C_k)X\bigr)^T\bigl((I - C_k)Y\bigr), \]

we have \(T_k(X,Y) = X^T A_k Y\) with

\[ A_k = (I - C_k)^T(I - C_k) = 2I - C_k - C_k^T. \]

Substituting \(R_k = X^T A_k Y\) into the regression estimator,

\[ \hat\sigma_{\mathrm{reg}} = H_{1\cdot} R = X^T A Y, \]

so the regression estimator is a bilinear form \(X^T A Y\) with

\[ A = \sum_{k=1}^L H_{1,k}\, A_k. \]

Since each \(A_k = 2I - C_k - C_k^T\) is a symmetric circulant, \(A\) is also a symmetric circulant, determined by its first row \(\tilde{a} \in \mathbb{R}^n\).

Conjecture. The first row \(\tilde{a}\) of the regression matrix \(A\) is the minimum-norm solution described in the previous section. That is, the regression approach and the Frobenius-norm minimization produce the same estimator.

4.8 Feasibility of the Regression Estimator

To show that \(\tilde{a}\) is the minimum-norm solution, we must verify it is feasible, i.e., that it satisfies the trace and unbiasedness constraints.

The regression estimator is the bilinear form \(X^T A Y\) with \(A = \sum_{k=1}^L c_k A_k\), where \(c_k = H_{1,k} = \frac{2L+1-3k}{nL(L-1)}\). Since we evaluate \(g(\ell)\) only for \(\ell \geq L\), every \(k \leq L\) satisfies \(k \leq \ell\), so the Lemma (see Other Mathematical Results) gives \(\mathbf{1}_\ell^T A_k \mathbf{1}_\ell = 2k\). By linearity, \[g(\ell) = \mathbf{1}_\ell^T A\, \mathbf{1}_\ell = 2\sum_{k=1}^L c_k k,\] which is independent of \(\ell\). Both constraints then reduce to evaluating \(\sum_k c_k\) and \(\sum_k c_k k\) via the closed form of \(c_k\).

Trace constraint: \(\operatorname{tr}(A) = 1\). Since \(\operatorname{tr}(A_k) = \operatorname{tr}(2I - C_k - C_k^T) = 2n\) (as \(C_k\) has no fixed points for \(k \geq 1\)), we have \(\operatorname{tr}(A) = 2n\sum_k c_k\). Applying the Gaussian sum \(\sum_{k=1}^L k = \frac{L(L+1)}{2}\), \[\sum_{k=1}^L c_k = \frac{1}{nL(L-1)}\left[(2L+1)L - \frac{3L(L+1)}{2}\right] = \frac{1}{n(L-1)}\cdot\frac{L-1}{2} = \frac{1}{2n}.\] Therefore \(\operatorname{tr}(A) = 2n \cdot \frac{1}{2n} = 1\). \(\square\)

Unbiasedness: \(g(\ell) = 0\) for \(\ell = L, \ldots, \lfloor n/2 \rfloor\). For \(\ell \geq L\), \(g(\ell) = 2\sum_k c_k k\). Applying the Gaussian sums \(\sum_{k=1}^L k = \frac{L(L+1)}{2}\) and \(\sum_{k=1}^L k^2 = \frac{L(L+1)(2L+1)}{6}\), \[\sum_{k=1}^L c_k k = \frac{1}{nL(L-1)}\left[(2L+1)\frac{L(L+1)}{2} - \frac{3L(L+1)(2L+1)}{6}\right] = \frac{(2L+1)L(L+1)}{nL(L-1)}\left[\frac{1}{2} - \frac{1}{2}\right] = 0.\] Therefore \(g(\ell) = 0\) for all \(\ell \geq L\). \(\square\)

4.9 The Regression Estimator is the Minimum-Norm Estimator

Having established feasibility, it remains to show that \(\tilde{a}\) is the minimum-norm feasible solution.

Setup. Since the unbiasedness constraints are homogeneous (\(Ga = 0\)), every feasible \(a\) lies in \(\mathrm{null}(G)\). The trace constraint \(\mathrm{tr}(A) = 1\) is the only inhomogeneous condition, cutting out an affine hyperplane within \(\mathrm{null}(G)\): \[F = \{a \in \mathrm{null}(G) : \mathrm{tr}(A) = 1\}.\] Let \(a^* \in F\) be the minimum-norm feasible solution. We will show \(a^* = \tilde{a}\) by writing \(a^* = \tilde{a} + w\) with \(w = a^* - \tilde{a}\) and expanding: \[\|a^*\|^2 = \|\tilde{a}\|^2 + \|w\|^2 + 2\langle\tilde{a}, w\rangle.\] Steps 1 and 2 below show \(\langle\tilde{a}, w\rangle = 0\), so the expansion reduces to \[\|a^*\|^2 = \|\tilde{a}\|^2 + \|w\|^2.\] Since \(a^*\) is optimal and \(\tilde{a}\) is feasible, \(\|a^*\|^2 \leq \|\tilde{a}\|^2\), so \[\|a^*\|^2 = \|\tilde{a}\|^2 + \|w\|^2 \leq \|\tilde{a}\|^2 \implies \|w\|^2 \leq 0.\] Therefore \(w = 0\) and \(a^* = \tilde{a}\).

Step 1: Characterize vectors satisfying \((*)\).

Since both \(a^*, \tilde{a} \in F\), the difference \(w = a^* - \tilde{a}\) satisfies \(Gw = 0\) (as both \(Ga^* = 0\) and \(G\tilde{a} = 0\)) and \(\mathrm{tr}(\mathrm{circ}(w)) = \mathrm{tr}(\mathrm{circ}(a^*)) - \mathrm{tr}(\mathrm{circ}(\tilde{a})) = 1 - 1 = 0\). Since \(\mathrm{tr}(\mathrm{circ}(w)) = nw_0\), we have \(w_0 = 0\). So \(w\) satisfies \[Gw = 0, \quad w_0 = 0. \tag{$*$}\]

Let \(w\) satisfy \((*)\). The constraint matrix \(G\) has entries \(G_{\ell,0} = \ell\) and \(G_{\ell,k} = 2(\ell-k)^+\) for \(k \geq 1\), so \(Gw = 0\) requires for each \(\ell = L, \ldots, \lfloor n/2 \rfloor\):

\[\ell\, w_0 + \sum_{k=1}^{\lfloor n/2 \rfloor} 2(\ell - k)^+\, w_k = 0.\]

We analyze this system by applying a first-difference operator twice. For any matrix \(B\) with \(r\) rows, define \(M(B)\) as the \((r-1) \times r\) bidiagonal matrix applied to \(B\):

\[M(B) = \begin{pmatrix} -1 & 1 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\ 0 & -1 & 1 & \cdots & 0 \\ \vdots & & \ddots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 0 & 0 & \cdots & -1 & 1 \end{pmatrix} B,\]

so that \(M(B)_\ell = B_{\ell+1} - B_\ell\). We write \(M^2G\) as shorthand for \(M(M(G))\) — applying the operator twice sequentially, each time with a smaller matrix of appropriate dimensions. Since \(Gw = 0\) implies \(M(G)w = 0\) and \(M(M(G))w = 0\), each application yields simpler conditions.

Step 1a: Show \(w_k = 0\) for \(k > L\).

First application (\(M(G)w = 0\)). Subtracting the \(\ell\)-th row from the \((\ell+1)\)-th gives

\[w_0 + 2\sum_{k=1}^{\ell} w_k = 0, \qquad \ell = L, \ldots, \lfloor n/2 \rfloor - 1.\]

The resulting matrix \(MG\) has a column of \(1\)s in position \(k=0\) and \(2\)s in positions \(k = 1, \ldots, \ell\) (filling in one more entry per row):

\[MG = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & \cdots & 2 & 0 & 0 & \cdots \\ 1 & 2 & \cdots & 2 & 2 & 0 & \cdots \\ 1 & 2 & \cdots & 2 & 2 & 2 & \cdots \\ \vdots & & & & & & \ddots \end{pmatrix}.\]

Second application (\(M(M(G))w = 0\)). Subtracting consecutive equations from \(M(G)w = 0\) gives \(2w_{\ell+1} = 0\) for each \(\ell = L, \ldots, \lfloor n/2 \rfloor - 2\), so \(w_k = 0\) for all \(k > L\).

The matrix \(M(M(G))\) has exactly one nonzero entry per row — a \(2\) in column \(\ell + 1\) — making the zero conditions on each \(w_k\) explicit:

\[M(M(G)) = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & \cdots & 0 & 2 & 0 & 0 & \cdots \\ 0 & \cdots & 0 & 0 & 2 & 0 & \cdots \\ 0 & \cdots & 0 & 0 & 0 & 2 & \cdots \\ \vdots & & & & & & \ddots \end{pmatrix}.\]

Step 1b: Characterize \((w_1, \ldots, w_L)\).

Having zeroed out \(w_k\) for \(k > L\), we need two conditions on the remaining entries \((w_1, \ldots, w_L)\). We obtain these by working back through \(MGw = 0\) and \(Gw = 0\) at \(\ell = L\) — each provides one independent condition, and no further applications of \(M\) are needed to characterize the null space.

Recall from Step 1a that \(M(G)w = 0\) gives \[w_0 + 2\sum_{k=1}^{\ell} w_k = 0, \qquad \ell = L, \ldots, \lfloor n/2 \rfloor - 1.\] Setting \(\ell = L\) and substituting \(w_k = 0\) for \(k > L\) gives \(w_0 + 2\sum_{k=1}^L w_k = 0\). Since \(w_0 = 0\) by \((*)\), this forces \(\sum_{k=1}^L w_k = 0\).

Similarly, recall from Step 1a that \(Gw = 0\) gives \(\ell\, w_0 + \sum_{k=1}^{\lfloor n/2 \rfloor} 2(\ell-k)^+\, w_k = 0\). Setting \(\ell = L\) and splitting the sum: \[L\, w_0 + \sum_{k=1}^{L} 2(L-k)^+\, w_k + \sum_{k=L+1}^{\lfloor n/2 \rfloor} 2(L-k)^+\, w_k = 0.\] The second sum vanishes since \((L-k)^+ = 0\) for \(k > L\), and \(w_0 = 0\) by \((*)\), leaving \(\sum_{k=1}^L 2(L-k)\, w_k = 0\). Expanding and applying \(\sum_{k=1}^L w_k = 0\) gives \(\sum_{k=1}^L k\, w_k = 0\).

Therefore the vectors satisfying \((*)\) are exactly those with \(w_0 = 0\), \(w_k = 0\) for \(k > L\), and \((w_1, \ldots, w_L)\) satisfying

\[\sum_{k=1}^L w_k = 0, \qquad \sum_{k=1}^L k\,w_k = 0.\]

To summarize, each level of differentiation contributes one layer of structure:

Equation Conclusion
\(M(M(G))w = 0\) \(w_k = 0\) for \(k > L\)
\(M(G)w = 0\) at \(\ell = L\) \(\sum_{k=1}^L w_k = 0\)
\(Gw = 0\) at \(\ell = L\) \(\sum_{k=1}^L k\, w_k = 0\)

Step 2: Show \(\tilde{a} \perp w\).

Since \(\tilde{a} = \sum_{k=1}^L c_k \tilde{a}_k\), by linearity of the inner product: \[\langle \tilde{a}, w \rangle = \sum_{k=1}^L c_k \langle \tilde{a}_k, w \rangle.\]

Since \(\tilde{a}_k = 2e_0 - e_k - e_{n-k}\), \[\langle \tilde{a}_k, w \rangle = 2w_0 - w_k - w_{n-k}.\]

From Step 1: \(w_0 = 0\), and for \(k \in \{1, \ldots, L\}\) we have \(n - k > L\), so \(w_{n-k} = 0\). Therefore \(\langle \tilde{a}_k, w \rangle = -w_k\), and \[\langle \tilde{a}, w \rangle = -\sum_{k=1}^L c_k\, w_k.\]

Since \(c_k = \frac{2L+1-3k}{nL(L-1)}\) is affine in \(k\),

\[\sum_{k=1}^L c_k\, w_k = \frac{2L+1}{nL(L-1)} \sum_{k=1}^L w_k - \frac{3}{nL(L-1)} \sum_{k=1}^L k\,w_k = 0,\]

where both sums vanish by Step 1. Therefore \(\langle \tilde{a}, w \rangle = 0\) for all \(w\) satisfying \((*)\). \(\square\)
